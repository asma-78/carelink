Dangers of Preservatives and Processed Food

Processed foods often contain a myriad of additives, including preservatives, artificial colors, and flavor enhancers, designed to extend shelf life and enhance taste. While convenient, the regular consumption of these foods can pose significant health risks.

Preservatives like nitrates, nitrites, and BHA/BHT have been linked to various health issues. Nitrates and nitrites, commonly found in cured meats, can form potentially carcinogenic compounds in the body. BHA and BHT, used to prevent spoilage in fats and oils, have raised concerns regarding their potential to interfere with endocrine function and contribute to tumor growth in animal studies.

Regular consumption of processed foods is associated with an increased risk of obesity, heart disease, diabetes, and certain types of cancer. They often lack the satiety provided by whole foods, leading to overeating and a vicious cycle of unhealthy eating habits.

NOW THAT WE HAVE SEEN THE DANGERS OF PRESERVATIVES AND GENETICALLY MODIFIED CROPS, LET'S LOOK AT HOW TEHNOLOGY CAN MAKE NATURAL FOOD MORE ACCESSIBLE

  1. Digital technology can transform food system in three ways. First, it can expand farmers’ access to capital and resources. With the touch of a button on their phone, farmers can now rent machines—like tractors—that require significant capital to buy

  2. Digital technology can also disrupt value chains through economies of scale, allowing smaller players to be integrated into the value chain. For example, e-commerce platforms can link producers directly to consumers, relaxing the constraint that producers need to be a certain size to reach customers.

  3. Finally, digital technology can disrupt the management of natural resources through precision tools, helping to boost food production sustainably. Digital technologies can make information on land, soils and other resources more widely available, allowing farmers to apply inputs like fertilizer and water in a more precise manner.

Here are real life success stories

  1. Twiga Foods, a Kenyan company founded in 2014, uses mobile technology to connect smallholder farmers directly with urban vendors, making fresh, natural produce more accessible and affordable. Through a simple app, vendors order fruits and vegetables, which Twiga collects from farms and delivers efficiently using data-driven logistics. This system reduces food waste, cuts out costly middlemen, and ensures farmers are paid quickly via mobile money. As a result, consumers get fresher food at lower prices, and farmers earn more, making natural food both accessible and sustainable.

  2. Guests at LSU AgCenter’s annual wheat and oat field day in April got the chance to attend a drone presentation. Discussion came up about how drones can be used to track the health of crops, especially in observation plots. “One of the things we’re thinking about in using drone data is that we have 600 or so plots that are observation plots and have DNA for all of them, which can be expensive. We wanted to learn if we could use drone information to link those observations together without the DNA data,” plant geneticist Noah DeWitt.

  3. Remote work is not something farmers have been able to consider in the past. But some technology can make it easier for farmers to work away from their land, whether that’s in their home next to their family, or while traveling. An app developed by Missouri farmer Nathan Holmes, Pump Trakr, works to manage irrigation through an on-farm communication tool. Farmers sign up to a subscription service to have real-time irrigation monitored and logged into a record.
